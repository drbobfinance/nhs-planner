ℹ️ Important context: Unlike households, governments don't need to "back" their debt with hard assets — they can tax, issue bonds, and create money. However, the UK's hard reserve position is weak compared to countries like Germany, Italy, or the US which hold far larger gold reserves relative to debt. The BoE's QE gilt holdings are the largest single "asset" but represent money already created, not independent wealth.
Why the UK "Holds $900bn of US Treasuries" — But It's NOT a Government Asset

You may have seen headlines stating the UK holds nearly $900 billion in US Treasury bonds — making it the second-largest foreign holder after Japan. This is technically true, but deeply misleading as a measure of UK government wealth.

The US Treasury data records holdings by country of custody, not by owner. The vast majority of those $900bn of Treasuries are held by London-based hedge funds — not by the UK government, the Bank of England, or the British public. London has become the world's largest hedge fund hub, and those funds use UK custodian banks to hold their US assets.

The actual UK government foreign currency reserves — held in the Exchange Equalisation Account (EEA) and managed by the Bank of England — total approximately £130bn. That is the figure on this page.

❌ NOT Government Assets
~$897bn (~£700bn)
US Treasuries held via London custody — privately owned by hedge funds. Cannot be used to pay UK national debt or fund public services.
✅ Actual Government Assets
~£130bn
HM Treasury Exchange Equalisation Account — gold, foreign currency, SDRs. These ARE the UK government's official reserves.
🏦 Why Does London Hold So Much?
London is the world's largest hedge fund hub. Funds use the "basis trade" — buying Treasuries while selling futures — to exploit tiny price differences. These strategies require holding large volumes of physical Treasuries in custodian accounts, which appear as "UK holdings" in US data.
