⚠️ THE OBR'S OWN WARNING: "SOMETHING HAS TO GIVE"

The Office for Budget Responsibility — the UK government's own official forecaster — projects that on current policy settings, public debt will rise to 274% of GDP by 2074. Public spending will reach 60%+ of GDP while revenues remain around 40%. The OBR states explicitly the public finances are on an unsustainable long-term path.

🚨 These four spending categories alone — debt interest, public sector pensions, NHS and state pension/welfare — are projected to consume over 35% of GDP by 2050. Total tax revenues today are only ~37% of GDP. This leaves almost nothing for defence, education, infrastructure, policing or any other public service. This is the fiscal cliff.

⚡ What "unsustainable" actually means in practice: Long before the 274% figure is reached, bond markets would react. UK gilt yields would rise sharply as investors demand higher compensation for risk — which itself accelerates the debt spiral (see our interest rate scenarios page). The IMF or financial markets would force a fiscal adjustment — either through austerity, higher taxes, inflation, or in extremis, debt restructuring. The OBR says explicitly: "something has to give."
What Could Change the Trajectory — Policy Levers Available
📈 Higher Growth
Each 0.5% extra annual GDP growth reduces debt/GDP by ~15pp by 2050. The OBR says returning to pre-2008 productivity trends would largely solve the problem.
🏥 NHS Reform
Healthcare is the single biggest long-term pressure. Containing NHS cost growth to GDP growth (rather than above it) would save ~8% of GDP by 2075.
👴 Pension Age
Raising state pension age to 70 by 2050 would save ~2% of GDP annually. Switching public sector pensions to defined contribution would cap future liabilities.
💷 Tax Rises
Raising tax/GDP ratio from 37% to 42% (Scandinavian levels) would broadly close the gap — but would require the largest peacetime tax rise in UK history.
✂️ Spending Cuts
Reducing public spending by 3–4% of GDP (about £90bn in today's terms) would stabilise debt — but would require cuts deeper than the 2010–2019 austerity programme.
📊 Combination
The OBR suggests a combination of higher growth, gradual pension reform, modest tax rises and spending efficiency — no single lever is sufficient alone.
✅ The OBR's conclusion: The UK's fiscal challenge is severe but not unique — most advanced economies face similar ageing pressures. The key difference is the starting level of debt (already ~94% of GDP) which leaves less room to absorb shocks before intervention is forced. Early action is significantly cheaper than delayed action.
