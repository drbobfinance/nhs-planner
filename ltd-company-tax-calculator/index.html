📖 What these strategies actually mean, in plain English

Salary vs dividends — what's the difference?

As a director of your own company, you can pay yourself two ways. A salary is a wage — it's a company expense (reduces Corporation Tax) but has Income Tax and National Insurance taken off, both for you and the company. A dividend is a payment to shareholders out of profit that's already been taxed at the Corporation Tax rate — you then pay dividend tax on top, but at lower rates than Income Tax, and with no National Insurance at all. That's why most director-shareholders take a small salary (enough to count towards their state pension) and the rest as dividends.

Corporation Tax

This is the tax your company pays on its profit, before anything is paid out to you. The rate depends on how much profit the company makes — smaller profits are taxed at a lower rate, larger ones at a higher rate, with a sliding scale in between.

Retaining profit in the company

You don't have to take all the profit out every year. Anything left in the company (after Corporation Tax) is called retained profit. This can be useful for two reasons: you can extract it later in a year when your personal income is lower (paying less dividend tax on it), and — importantly for NHS staff — money left in the company doesn't count as your personal income at all, so it has no effect on your Annual Allowance tapering. See our guide on whether a Ltd company makes sense for you for more on this.

Spouse/family dividend splitting

If your spouse or an adult family member genuinely holds shares in the company, dividends can be paid to them too — using their tax-free allowance and lower tax bands instead of it all landing on your higher rate. This can save real money, but HMRC watches this closely (see the warning box further down) — it needs to be a genuine shareholding, not just a name on paper.

Company pension contribution

Instead of paying yourself and then contributing to a pension personally, the company can pay directly into your SIPP. It's a company expense (reduces Corporation Tax), and nothing is deducted from it on the way in — but it still counts towards your Annual Allowance. See our Annual Allowance calculator to check your headroom first.

Director's loan

If you take money out of the company that isn't salary, dividend or expenses, it's treated as a loan from the company to you. Small, short-term loans are fine, but over £10,000 they create a taxable "benefit in kind," and if not repaid within 9 months of the company's year end, the company faces an extra tax charge (called a "s455 charge") until it's repaid.
