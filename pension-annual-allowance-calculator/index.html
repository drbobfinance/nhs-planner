⚙️ NHS Pension Annual Allowance Calculator

Correct NHS PIA formula, with tapering, carry forward and a month-by-month breach projection

Guidance only — not financial advice. NHS pension Annual Allowance calculations are genuinely complex. Always cross-check this estimate against your Annual Benefit Statement at totalrewardstatements.nhs.uk.

📖 What is the Annual Allowance, in plain English?

Every year, there's a limit on how much your pension is allowed to grow before extra tax kicks in. That limit is called the Annual Allowance (AA). For most people it's currently £60,000 a year. It isn't about how much you personally pay in — with a final-salary-style scheme like the NHS pension, it's about how much your future annual pension increased in value over the year, converted into a notional "contribution" figure called your Pension Input Amount (PIA).

The NHS pension is unusually generous, which is great for your retirement — but it also means the PIA can grow faster than people expect, especially after a promotion, a big jump in overtime/PAs, or a pay rise. If your PIA for the year is higher than your Annual Allowance, you don't get taxed on the excess directly — instead it gets added to your income and taxed at your marginal rate as an Annual Allowance charge. This can come as a surprise because it isn't shown on your monthly payslip; it only shows up when your Annual Benefit Statement lands or HMRC catches up with you.

Three things can reduce how much allowance you actually have: tapering (explained below), having no carry forward left from previous years, and simply having a PIA that's grown a lot in one go.

📖 What does "adjusted income" actually mean?

This is the single most misunderstood part of the whole system, so here's the plain version. There are two different income figures HMRC looks at:

Threshold income — roughly speaking, your total taxable income for the year (salary, private practice, dividends, rental income etc.) minus any personal pension contributions you made yourself. This is the first test: if threshold income is £200,000 or below, tapering doesn't apply to you at all, no matter how high your adjusted income is.
Adjusted income — your threshold income, but with your employer's pension contributions added back on top (for NHS staff, that's the NHS's own contribution, currently around 20.68% of your pensionable pay — a substantial amount, since the NHS scheme is generous). This is the second test: if adjusted income is above £260,000, tapering starts to bite.

Worked example: Say your NHS salary is £120,000 and you earn £90,000 from private practice, with no personal pension contributions of your own. Threshold income = £210,000 (above £200,000, so tapering is in play). The NHS employer contribution on your £120,000 salary is about £24,800. Adjusted income = £210,000 + £24,800 = £234,800 — below £260,000, so in this example tapering still wouldn't apply. But add another £30,000 of private practice income and adjusted income tips over £260,000, and your Annual Allowance starts shrinking by £1 for every £2 above that — down to a floor of £10,000.

📖 Paying into a private pension from a Ltd company — what does that mean?

If you have private practice or locum income running through a limited company (see our Ltd Company Tax Optimiser), the company itself can pay money directly into a personal pension (a SIPP, typically) on your behalf — this is called an employer pension contribution, even though "employer" here just means your own company.

Why bother? Three reasons: (1) it's a genuine business expense, so it reduces the company's profit and therefore its Corporation Tax bill — fully tax-deductible; (2) unlike paying yourself a salary or dividend first and then contributing personally, there's no Income Tax, dividend tax or National Insurance charged on the way — the money goes straight from company profit into your pension; (3) it counts towards your Annual Allowance headroom just like any other pension contribution, so if you have unused allowance (see your result below), this is usually the single most tax-efficient way to use it.

The catch: it still uses up your Annual Allowance for the year, same as everything else. If you've already used up your allowance through NHS pension growth alone, adding more via the company would trigger an AA charge rather than saving tax — so it only makes sense once you know your headroom, which is exactly what the calculator below works out.

📖 Can I "buy extra years" in the NHS pension?

Not quite "extra years" any more — that language is left over from the older 1995 Section scheme. If you're in the 2015 Scheme (most current staff), the equivalent option is called Additional Pension: you can buy a fixed amount of extra annual pension (paid from retirement onwards), purchased either as a one-off lump sum or as ongoing contributions over a set number of years, up to a maximum amount set by NHS Pensions each scheme year.

There's also a separate option called ERRBO (Early Retirement Reduction Buy Out), which reduces the penalty for retiring before your normal pension age, rather than increasing your pension itself.

Two important points: buying Additional Pension does count towards your Annual Allowance for the year you pay it (whether lump sum or spread), so it's subject to exactly the same headroom limits as everything else on this page. Applications and up-to-date costs are handled directly through NHS Pensions (NHSBSA).

Why can't this site just calculate the cost for you?

Every other figure on this site — take-home pay, tax, National Insurance — is worked out from fixed, published rates that apply to everyone equally. Additional Pension isn't priced that way. NHS Pensions works out what it costs you personally using actuarial factors: tables built by actuaries (specialists in pricing long-term financial risk) that estimate, roughly speaking, how many years you're likely to draw the pension for and how that money grows or is discounted in the meantime.

In practice that means the cost of buying, say, £1,000 a year of extra pension depends on:

  • Your age when you buy it — younger buyers pay less per £1,000 of pension, because NHS Pensions assumes there's longer for the "investment" to grow before you draw it, and it has more years of your ongoing contributions to work with if you're spreading the cost.
  • How you pay — a one-off lump sum is priced differently to spreading the cost as monthly contributions over several years.
  • The scheme's assumptions at the time — the actuarial tables themselves are reviewed and updated by the scheme's actuaries periodically (life expectancy assumptions, discount rates and so on), so the same purchase can cost a different amount from one year to the next even for someone the same age.

Because those tables aren't public in a form we could safely embed here, showing you a made-up number would risk being wrong for your actual circumstances — which matters for a decision that isn't easily reversible. That's why we point you to NHS Pensions directly for a genuine, personalised quote, rather than guessing.

It's worth getting a specialist adviser's view alongside that quote before committing, since buying Additional Pension is a long-term decision and hard to undo once started.
